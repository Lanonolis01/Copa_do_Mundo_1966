Prologue

The 1966 FIFA World Cup was held in England and featured the host team as champion for the first time. In the final, England defeated West Germany 4-2 in extra time, in a match marked by Geoff Hurst's famous 'ghost goal'. The tournament was also known for Pelé's great performance, despite him suffering many fouls and being eliminated early with the Brazilian National Football Team.

Introduction

In the year 1966, the world was experiencing a period of significant technological advances and cultural changes, with particular emphasis on the growth of television, telecommunications and space exploration. These advances brought people and countries closer together, allowing events such as the 1966 FIFA World Cup to be followed by millions of people around the world, demonstrating how technology was beginning to transform communication and entertainment.

Global Transmission via Satellite

The 1966 World Cup was the first to utilize satellite transmissions on a large scale, marking the beginning of the global television coverage of football. The distribution of images was made possible by newly launched satellites, such as Intelsat I (nicknamed Early Bird), the first commercial telecommunications satellite in the world. Before this, images reached other continents through aerial transport of film reels, delaying their broadcast by days. With satellites, European broadcasters were able to send signals quickly — some even live — to the Americas.

The Invention of the Replay

Today it is impossible to imagine a football match without reviewing the goals or the controversial plays, but it was in 1966 that this became a reality for the general public. The BBC implemented a rudimentary videotape system that allowed them to replay the best moments just a few seconds after they happened. Along with the replay, slow motion also emerged. Ironically, this technology was fundamental in the final itself: Geoff Hurst's iconic 'ghost goal' was relentlessly replayed to try to determine whether the ball had crossed the goal line.

The Transition to Color Broadcasting

The absolute majority of the public watched the 1966 World Cup on black and white televisions — color TV would only make its big debut in the 1970 World Cup in Mexico. Even so, 1966 served as a laboratory for this technology. Several cinema cameras were scattered throughout the stadiums running high-resolution color film, material that was used for the official documentary 'Goal!' (1966). Released in theaters, it was the first time that millions of fans around the world could see the uniforms and the green of the pitch of a World Cup with total clarity.

DRAM

DRAM (Dynamic Random Access Memory) was invented by Robert H. Dennard, an IBM researcher, and is considered the precursor to modern RAM. It stores data temporarily, keeping information that will be used for a short time and discarding it afterward. Before DRAM, computers were huge, slow, and extremely expensive. Dennard created a system that could store information using just one transistor per bit, making computers smaller, faster, and more affordable — a fundamental breakthrough for all of modern computing.

ARPANET

ARPANET was developed by researchers from the American agency ARPA, with notable contributions from Bob Taylor and Larry Roberts, and is considered the precursor to the modern internet. Its objective was to connect computers from universities and military centers, enabling fast and secure information exchange. In the context of the Cold War, the United States wanted a network resistant to failures and attacks: in 1966 they began planning the project, which later became the foundation of the modern internet. The first real connection of the network occurred in 1969.

Pocket Calculator

The pocket calculator (Pocket Calculator) was created by a team from Texas Instruments formed by Jack Kilby, Jerry Merryman and James Van Tassel. The objective was to replace manual calculations and the enormous calculation machines that existed at the time. Before it, calculators were too large to be transported easily, which limited their use. With the portable version, electronic calculators became common in schools, offices and companies around the world.

ETEC Jorge Street

Project developed for educational purposes.

The idea emerged from a school activity, with the objective of promoting geographical and historical information about the World Cups, with the 1966 edition being the main theme of our work. The project was developed based on what we were taught in class, both in terms of website creation and the research conducted.
